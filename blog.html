Horizontal vs. Vertical Team Structure

Tags: wagie

TL;DR: More like what don't I do here

Preface: This is not about ORGANIZATION structure where there are already tons of articles around a trendy way of having a "no-hierarchy" org which I do not find very helpful since in my humble experience, hierarchical structures will form regardless of formal definition in an internal employee reporting chain. This is about IC-first teams operating in different modes within a larger organization.

Most teams I have been on have this structure, more or less with some exceptions that are not particularly important for the purposes of this post:
(PM) Product Manager: Handles direction (customer feedback loops, strategic paths), consults with other workstreams, major part in organizing incoming work items.
(EM) Engineering Manager: Can be involved in tactical paths with senior ICs, day-to-day soft skills with, well, managing ICs.
(TL) Tech Lead: Highly involved in both consulting the feasibility and development cost when evaluating strategic and tactical paths, may do architectural design.
(IC) Individual Contributor: Mostly there to churn out code.

This, in my limited experience, is a vertical team structure. Each member has a very narrow set of responsibilities which means they can specialize without having to over-extend their realm of competency. In this structure, one of the main bottlenecks is communication, since all work has to go through multiple units in order to eventually be done by the IC. In extreme cases, this becomes excruciating, particularly for senior ICs with high capacity for cross-discipline work.

Digression: not-quite-hyperbole of a highly vertical team. The PM starts a cycle of new work intake. This reconciles their strategy with evidence such as retrospective and forward-looking feedback i.e. customer complaints and direct feature asks. This incoming strategy is discussed with the TL in order to set feasibility and expected dev cost for prioritization purposes. Once the strategy is set, the EM and TL can start work on the tactical paths for delivering the larger strategic goal. Then, work is portioned out in as large or small tasks as the ICs are comfortable with and they work to deliver.

How to make performance review not suck

Tags: productivity

TL;DR: babysit yourself

I hear a lot of complaints around performance review, particularly that it is difficult to remember tasks completed, time consuming to report, and generally annoying (isn't it my manager's job to know everything that I've done and make a little report and send it for promo automagically? (no.))
You are responsible for your own tracking, accountability, and upwards reporting as part of being a high functioning autonomous IC, which is what we should all strive for. (digression: high autonomy IC is the goal)

If you followed the steps in Async Standups, then you already have the framework for any performance review. Subdivide your time into relevant frames, typically [day, week, quarter] to track and filter notable achievements. Each frame should only take a few minutes to complete. I have wrapper routines around my tasks in order to make them more efficient and keep in a good uninterrupted flow. This not only allows accomplishments to bubble up, but also gameifies my day leading to a better sense of accomplishment and overall contentment.

My optimal taskflow wrapping is:
Reference daily goals to set task
??? task ???
Record that the task is done both externally (i.e. outward facing work tracking apps) and then internally on my todo tracker.
Human necessary tasks (water intake / output, mobility operation, timeboxed existential crisis, snacks, etc.)

By making tracking part of your workflow, you avoid having to scramble later to remember all the tasks you completed. This works particularly well for tasks that are not easily tracked or that do not have artifacts (digression: Horizontal Org work; wtf am I doing?) and while it may be nice to just use a query on your work tracker (get where assignee=me and completed>-3months) I still prefer tracking in this way for the following reasons:
Not all work is accurately tracked in the software
Work item documentation can vary wildly
Not all things I do are tracked (don't argue that they should be)

By implementing quick self-tracking tasks, I have increased my daily flow and made it much easier to track and report performance.

Async Standups

Tags: productivity

TL;DR: You should have your standup meetings asynchronously in chat.

Most of the time spent in standup meetings is wasted. Here's a simple test you can do: at the end of your next synchronous standup meeting, test either yourself (or another team member, if you're feeling spicy) on another's update. Most likely, they will not remember and even if they do, they probably don't care. ICs are unlikely to sit idle, which means they will be distractedly working at best or just killing time after being interrupted from focus time.

Standups quickly turn into a productivity check which isn't relevant for anyone other than your Engineering Manager, and even then a 15-30 minute meeting every day to check that your engineers are doing their job is a waste of time. EMs should be able to trust ICs at a daily timescale.
The standup meeting should be reduced to the most relevant info in order of importance:

0. Asks for the team i.e. code reviews, general questions (though you should wonder why this wasn't asked async in team chat)
1. Changes since last standup or that are planned for the day that directly affect the team i.e. API updates
2. Adhoc discussions, preferably as breakouts with only those relevant to the conversation

Enjoy getting 15-30+ minutes back per day.
